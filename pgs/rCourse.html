R Learning Content

This additional learning content aims to enable you to:

  • Refresh and extend your knowledge, skills and critical understanding of advanced research methods.
  • Creatively apply the principles of quantitative research methods to research and practice.
  • Refresh and extend your skills in project design, management, analysis and presentation.

This content is also designed to help prepare you for any specific research projects (Dissertation) involved in undergraduate study(final year), masters level and a important aspect of doctoral level PhD programmes. This additional content should aid to ensure that you have the required knowledge and skills to successfully engage with those modules/projects.

The key foci for this content include:

  • Basic research concepts revision.
  • Research design and development.
  • Data processing and analysis.

The additional content is taught using a variety of techniques we hope (if enganged with) will provide a gradual means to improve your knowledge, understanding and approach to research theory and methods. This content may include include lectures, guided statistical analysis and tutorials with added task books and examples for practicing.

Introducing R Programming for Research


What is R?



R is an open-source programming language, which can be freely downloaded. At first downloading R alone will lead you to a command-line interface devoid of any clues for what you should do next (It's pretty scary). Thankfully, there is also freely available and widely used application, or IDE (intergrated development environment), called RStudio. Rstudio provides a much more friendly user interface and includes number of features to help write code. It is very much recommended to write your R code in RStudio. RStudio also has a lot of online tutorials on their website to help you get familiar with both R and the RStudio IDE.

There is no doubt that the learning curve for R is much steeper than producing one or two charts in a spreadsheet program like excel, google sheets, whatever.. However, there are real long-term advantages to learning a programming language and dedicated data processing and analysis tool such as R. Such advice to learn a programming language can seem both daunting and pretty vague, especially if you do not really understand what it means to code.

If you have never read or written any code before, it is difficult to know what the theoretical differences between spreadsheets and coding mean in practice. Therefore, I want to provide an example of analyzing and visualizing data in R. Hopefully, this will also demonstrate that, because R is open source and has a extremely large communite contributing various pre built packages, functions and code, you can actually get an extremely large amount accomplished without really needing to do any complex programming, and simply asking for what you want! :)
